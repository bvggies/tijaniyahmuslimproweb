No countdowns. No fireworks. Just a new beginning — simple, sacred and full of unseen potential.

Many people let this moment pass without notice. But for those paying attention, the start of the Islamic New Year is a gift. It invites us to start over with intention, not pressure. With presence, not perfection. It’s a time to realign with what truly matters.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ called this month “the month of Allah”. No other month in the calendar carries this title, a reminder of Muharram’s spiritual weight.

“The best of fasting after Ramadan is in the Month of Allah, al-Muharram.”
 — Sahih Muslim

This is the season for inward work. And one of the most powerful ways to honour it is by setting spiritual goals that are not only meaningful, but sustainable.

Begin the Islamic New Year With Reflection

Before setting goals, take a pause. Reflect on the past year. What brought you closer to Allah? What distracted you?

This isn’t about guilt. It’s about reconnecting with your heart. Think of it as a spiritual muhasabah — a personal check-in. Ask yourself gentle, guiding questions:

  • What part of my faith do I want to grow in?
  • Where did I feel most spiritually grounded?
  • What small practice am I ready to return to?

This honest reflection helps ensure your new goals come from sincerity — not comparison or social pressure.

Choose One Area to Grow (Not All of Them)

During Muharram, the energy of new beginnings can tempt us to change everything at once. But in Islam, lasting change is rooted in consistency.

Rather than overhauling your routine, choose one spiritual focus for this Islamic New Year. It could be:

  • Memorising a single surah
  • Praying sunnah prayers consistently
  • Fasting every Monday (starting with Ashura, the 10th of Muharram)
  • Spending 5 minutes with the Qur’an daily

Whatever you choose, start small. The Prophet ﷺ said the most beloved actions to Allah are those done consistently, even if they are few.

If you’re not sure where to begin, explore resources that support gradual growth. For example, Quran Lesson Cards inside the Muslim Pro app help you absorb meaning one verse at a time — making Quran study feel accessible and less overwhelming.

Honour the Sacredness of Ashura

One of the most spiritually significant days in Muharram is the Day of Ashura — the 10th of the month. It commemorates the day Allah saved Prophet Musa and his people from Pharaoh’s oppression. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ fasted on this day and encouraged others to do the same.

“Fasting the day of Ashura, I hope Allah will expiate for the sins of the previous year.”
— Sahih Muslim

He also mentioned that if he lived another year, he would fast on the 9th as well. This is why many Muslims fast on the 9th and 10th or the 10th and 11th of Muharram.

Fasting on Ashura is not just about reward — it’s about reconnecting with a legacy of resilience, gratitude, and divine rescue. For those beginning their spiritual journey anew, this fast is a beautiful way to mark the Islamic New Year with sincerity and presence.

Stay Spiritually Nourished Throughout the Week

Goals often fade when we feel spiritually disconnected. To stay grounded, build moments of reflection into your weekly rhythm.

You don’t need a full schedule of classes or commitments. Just one piece of meaningful Islamic content each week can keep your heart engaged.

Whether it’s watching a documentary about the Prophets, listening to a reflection on Ashura, or hearing stories from Muslims around the world — choose content that moves you and reminds you why you began.

Platforms like Qalbox on the Muslim Pro app offer short, insightful videos that complement your spiritual goals and help you stay anchored throughout Muharram and beyond.

Write It Down, Even If It’s Just a Line

Intentions are seeds. But without care, they fade. Writing down your spiritual goals — even briefly — helps them stay alive.

Try journaling at the end of each day. Not as a chore, but as a private conversation with yourself and with Allah. Write a short du’a. Note what you struggled with. Celebrate a small success. Keep it real, not perfect.

Tools like the Journal inside Muslim Pro can support this habit. It offers a private space to track your ibadah and reflect on your progress — without pressure or judgment.

Don’t Wait for Perfection. Begin With Intention.

You may set a goal and stumble. You might forget to fast on Ashura or miss a day of Qur’an. That’s okay.

Faith isn’t linear. It’s about returning, again and again.

“Indeed, those who say, ‘Our Lord is Allah’ and then remain steadfast — the angels will descend upon them…”
— Quran 41:30

So as Muharram 2025 begins, start gently. Set one goal. Make one du’a. Fast on Ashura if you can. And most importantly, anchor every step in your intention to grow closer to Allah.

You don’t need to do everything. You just need to begin.

]]> https://www.muslimpro.com/spiritual-goals-in-muharram/feed/ 0 32306 Dhul Hijjah and the Spirit of Restraint & Self-Discipline https://www.muslimpro.com/dhul-hijjah-and-the-spirit-of-restraint/ https://www.muslimpro.com/dhul-hijjah-and-the-spirit-of-restraint/#respond Sun, 15 Jun 2025 00:30:00 +0000 https://www.muslimpro.com/?p=29658

Eid al-Adha is more than a celebration. It is a moment of profound remembrance — of a father’s obedience, a son’s surrender and a test that changed the course of human faith. But beyond the rituals of Qurban lies a deeper question: What does sacrifice actually mean in our everyday lives?

We often associate sacrifice with something grand — giving up wealth, comfort, or safety for a greater cause. But for most of us, the real challenge isn’t found in dramatic acts. It’s in the quiet choices we make daily — to hold our tongue, to control our ego, to walk away from sin when no one is watching.

And that’s where restraint becomes ibadah.

The Legacy of Sacrifice Begins With Obedience

When Prophet Ibrahim عليه السلام was shown a vision to sacrifice his son, he didn’t argue. He didn’t delay. He didn’t ask for signs. He obeyed.

“So when they had both submitted their wills to Allah, and he laid him down upon his forehead, We called out to him, ‘O Ibrahim! You have fulfilled the vision. Indeed, this is how We reward the good-doers’” (Quran 37:103–105)

It was never about the act itself. It was about the willingness to surrender.

That same willingness is asked of us every day. When we’re tempted to respond in anger. When our desires pull us away from our salah. When we’re called to give up comfort for principle.

Real sacrifice today looks like restraint — saying no to the self so you can say yes to Allah.

Restraining the Nafs: A Daily Test

The nafs — our lower self — constantly seeks ease, praise, indulgence, and immediate gratification. It doesn’t like to be challenged. It resists change. But the path to Allah is not paved with comfort. It’s shaped by the moments when we choose Him over ourselves.

We stay up late for work, skip meals to meet deadlines, spend money on things we don’t need — all in the name of success or pleasure. But when it comes to sacrificing sleep for Fajr, or our ego for forgiveness, or luxury for modesty — suddenly, it feels too difficult.

That tension is where the test lies.

“Have you seen the one who takes his own desire as his god…?” (Quran 45:23)

True discipline means putting Allah above our impulses. Restraining the nafs isn’t about deprivation — it’s about elevation. It is choosing Jannah over instant gratification, and that choice, repeated daily, is ibadah.

Strength in Restraint

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ said: “The strong is not the one who overcomes others by force, but the one who controls himself in anger.” (Bukhari)

This hadith redefines strength. It’s not in outbursts or victories — but in quiet control.

When you resist gossip, when you walk away from temptation, when you lower your voice instead of raising it — that is spiritual strength. That is taqwa in action.

Discipline in Islam is not about perfection. It’s about persistence. Trying, failing, and trying again — always with the intention of drawing closer to Allah.

And that trying? That’s ibadah too.

Practicing Sacrifice in Daily Life

You don’t need grand gestures to embody the spirit of sacrifice. You need sincerity, intention, and quiet choices made with Allah in mind.

  • Wake up for Fajr even when your body pleads for more sleep. That’s your comfort — sacrificed.
  • Lower your gaze when your eyes want to wander. That’s your desire — restrained.
  • Give in charity even when your funds feel tight. That’s your attachment — released.
  • Choose silence when your anger wants to speak. That’s your pride — softened.

These moments may feel small, but they are deeply seen by Allah.

“Indeed, those who fear Allah in private — theirs will be forgiveness and a great reward.” (Quran 67:12)

Building a Lifestyle Rooted in Taqwa

Qurban happens once a year. But the opportunity to train the heart in restraint happens every single day.

Start with one change. One habit. One challenge. Maybe it’s fasting once a week. Maybe it’s cutting back on digital distractions. Maybe it’s choosing dhikr over scrolling. The shift doesn’t have to be big — it just has to be intentional.

And over time, that intention becomes rhythm. That rhythm becomes lifestyle. And that lifestyle, when anchored in sincerity, becomes ibadah.

The Heart of Qurban Lives in You

Sacrifice isn’t always visible. It’s not always about animals, rituals, or grand decisions. Sometimes, it’s in the unseen — the battles within the heart, the quiet choices made when no one’s watching.

“Their meat will not reach Allah, nor will their blood, but what reaches Him is piety from you.” (Quran 22:37)

That is what Qurban was always about. Not the act — but the sincerity behind it.

So as the days pass and the season of Dhul Hijjah fades, let its lessons remain. Let restraint become your daily ibadah. Let discipline be the thread that keeps you tethered to your Rabb. And let every quiet yes to Allah shape the person you are becoming.

Continue your sadaqah this Dhul Hijjah — and let your impact multiply. Whether it’s food for the hungry, essentials for children, or aid for those in crisis, every act of giving is a step toward lasting baraqah.

]]> https://www.muslimpro.com/a-letter-to-every-muslim-woman/feed/ 0 29647 Islam Doesn’t Just Tolerate Diversity — It Honours It https://www.muslimpro.com/islam-honours-diversity/ https://www.muslimpro.com/islam-honours-diversity/#respond Wed, 11 Jun 2025 00:30:00 +0000 https://www.muslimpro.com/?p=29634

When the Prophet Muhammad صلى الله عليه وسلم stood before more than 100,000 people in his Farewell Sermon, he delivered a message that cut through centuries of tribalism, racism, and social hierarchy:

“No Arab has superiority over a non-Arab, nor a non-Arab over an Arab; no white over a black, nor black over a white, except by piety and good action.”

It wasn’t just revolutionary for 7th-century Arabia. It is still revolutionary today. In a world where difference is often used as a weapon, the Prophet’s words were a healing balm. Islam doesn’t just tolerate diversity — it honours it. It elevates it. It cherishes it as a sign of Allah’s mercy.

The Qur’an echoes this message with beautiful clarity:

“O mankind, indeed We have created you from male and female and made you peoples and tribes that you may know one another. Indeed, the most noble of you in the sight of Allah is the most righteous of you.” (Quran 49:13)

These aren’t just lofty ideals. They are divine instructions: a reminder that our differences are not faults, but features of a deeply interconnected design.

Living the Principle in Everyday Life

Despite these clear teachings, many Muslims still find it difficult to extend this honouring of diversity in daily interactions. We may unintentionally hold biases around language, culture, or appearances. We may feel closer to those who look or sound like us, and more hesitant toward those who don’t.

But Islam encourages us to rise above these instincts. The Prophet SAW said: “Verily, Allah does not look at your appearance or wealth, but rather He looks at your hearts and actions.” (Muslim)

Every Muslim — regardless of background, accent, or skin colour — stands equally before Allah. And it is taqwa, not tribe or title, that defines our rank.

The Prophet’s Example of Embracing Difference

The Prophet صلى الله عليه وسلم didn’t just teach inclusion — he practised it. He appointed Bilal ibn Rabah, a man of African descent and former slave, as the first to call the adhan. He honoured Salman al-Farsi, a Persian who endured slavery and years of searching for spiritual truth. When Salman proposed the trench strategy during the Battle of the Trench, the Prophet صلى الله عليه وسلم embraced his idea which was a pivotal moment that helped secure Madinah. 

He also deeply respected Al-Najashi, the Christian king of Abyssinia who gave protection to the early Muslims when they were persecuted in Makkah. Though not Muslim at first, the Prophet SAW described him as a just ruler and prayed for him upon his death.

Each companion came from a different background, but all were valued equally. The Prophet SAW saw beyond their origins. He saw their faith, character, and sincerity.

Reflection for Today

We may not control the structures around us, but we can control our adab — our manners, our assumptions, and our way of seeing others.

Do we make room for different cultures in our events and homes? Do we listen to someone who worships differently than we’re used to? Do we value sincerity over style, taqwa over fluency?

These are quiet ways to honour the diversity Allah created. These are subtle forms of ibadah too.

A More Welcoming Ummah Starts With Us

The Prophet’s final message was not just a farewell. It was a legacy. His sermon is a reminder that we each have a role to play in building a welcoming, spiritually rich ummah.

From community gatherings to classroom settings, from how we greet others in the masjid to who we give leadership opportunities to — each choice reflects whether we’re living the Sermon’s message or leaving it behind.

More Than Tolerance

Islam doesn’t ask us to erase differences. It asks us to see Allah’s design in them. To learn from one another. To strengthen bonds through shared faith, not sameness.

Every time we choose humility over judgement, inclusion over assumption, and sincerity over appearance, we honour the Prophet’s final words.

Let us not just recall the sermon. Let us live it.

His conversation with Ismail AS, who also showed remarkable willingness to submit to the divine decree, is a testament to incredible faith.

“When Ibrahim AS said to his son: ‘O my son, indeed I have seen in a dream that I [must] sacrifice you. So see what you think.’ Ismail replied, ‘O my father, do as you are commanded. You will find me, if Allah wills, among the patient.’ (Quran 37:102)

This was the ultimate  test of Ikhlas: To be willing to surrender what was most precious, to obey a command that seemed humanly impossible, purely out of love, trust, and surrender to Allah’s will. There was no worldly benefit to gain, no human validation to seek, only the absolute purity of intention to please the Creator. Allah’s intervention, replacing Ismail AS with a ram, proved that sincerity and willingness to obey were the ultimate measure of their Ikhlas, not the physical act of Qurban itself.

Connecting this incredible story to our lives today, our test of Ikhlas might not involve such a dramatic command, but it exists in many forms. It’s choosing to spend an hour helping a neighbor purely for Allah’s sake, even when you could be scrolling through social media. It’s donating to charity anonymously instead of seeking public recognition. It’s striving for excellence in your work because it is a trust from Allah, not just to impress your boss.

The Qurban sacrifice, by reminding us of Prophet Ibrahim’s ultimate sincerity, highlights that our willingness to sacrifice for Allah, whether wealth, time, or comfort, purely for His sake, is where true devotion lies.

This act trains us to detach from wealth and give purely for Allah.

  3. Distributing the Meat: Sharing the blessed meat with family, friends, and especially the needy, is a core part of Qurban. Ikhlas are crucial here. Are we distributing seeking praise for our generosity, or solely to fulfill Allah’s command and bring joy and sustenance to others purely for His sake?
