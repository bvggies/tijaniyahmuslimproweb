In Palestine, some olive trees have lived for thousands of years. They have watched over generations of families during times of prayer, harvest, and hardship. More than just trees, they are living symbols of faith and resilience. 

This article explores how the blessed olive tree teaches us profound lessons about hope and unwavering trust in Allah, drawn from the Quran and the wisdom of the Prophet ﷺ.

The Olive Tree Whispers Stories of Generations

There’s a tree that has given shade and fruit to great-grandparents, and to their grandparents before them. For countless Palestinian families, the olive tree is just that. It is a silent member of the family. Its roots run deep in the soil, just as family histories run deep in the heart.

The olive tree is so important to Palestinian identity because it represents a deep, unbreakable connection to their ancestral land, family heritage, and economic livelihood. 

These ancient trees are living monuments, carrying centuries of history and quiet strength. Each harvest is more than olives. It gathers people in work, stories, and prayer. To lose a tree is to lose a living record of that heritage. 

For Muslims in other parts of the world, even seeing these trees from afar keeps us tied to a shared legacy.

The Quran Describes the Olive Tree as a Source of Divine Light

The olive tree holds a special place in Islam. Allah honors it in the Quran, most beautifully in Surah An-Nur. In a powerful parable about His divine light, Allah says:

“Its oil would almost glow, even if untouched by fire. Light upon light! Allah guides His light whom He wills.” (Surah An-Nur, 24:35)

The verse describes the source of this oil as a blessed olive tree, which is “neither of the east nor of the west.” Many scholars explain this beautiful phrase to mean that its light is not limited to one place or time; it is a universal blessing, pure and accessible to all who seek it. The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ also encouraged us to use olive oil, connecting its physical nourishment to a deeper spiritual blessing.

It Is Also a Witness to Uprooting and Hardship

For generations, Palestinians have seen their sacred olive groves uprooted and destroyed. This is more than an economic loss. It is an attempt to erase memory and identity.

Each tree that is lost represents a severed link to family history and heritage. It is a painful symbol of the hardship faced by the Palestinian people. Yet, even in the face of this loss, the olive tree continues to teach a lesson in patience and resilience.

Allah Promises That He Is Never Unaware

During times of immense difficulty, it can be hard to hold onto hope. However, the Quran offers powerful comfort and a reminder of divine justice. Allah assures the believers:

“And never think that Allah is unaware of what the wrongdoers do.” (Surah Ibrahim, 14:42)

This verse is a source of great sabr (patience) for those suffering injustice. It is a promise that no pain or struggle goes unseen by Allah. This certainty in divine awareness strengthens faith and provides the spiritual fortitude to endure.

We Are Commanded to Choose Hope and Plant for the Future

Islam teaches us to be people of action and hope, even in the darkest of times. The most powerful example of this comes from a hadith of the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ, which teaches us to maintain hope until the very last moment. The Prophet ﷺ said:

“If the Hour (the Day of Resurrection) is about to be established and one of you was holding a palm shoot, let him take advantage of even one second before the Hour is established to plant it.” (Musnad Ahmad)

This teaching is the ultimate act of hope. It commands us to build, cultivate, and plan for the future, trusting completely in Allah’s plan, even when the end seems near. Planting a sapling in the face of destruction is a powerful declaration that hope will not be uprooted.

Conclusion

The olive tree of Palestine is a teacher of faith. It shows us that roots of heritage can withstand the harshest storms. It reminds us, through the words of the Quran, that divine light is a universal blessing. And through the wisdom of the Prophet ﷺ, it teaches us that our duty is to always plant seeds of hope for the future.

Some answers are clear from the Quran and Sunnah, while others involve modern scenarios. Understanding these rulings helps you protect your worship and observe fasting with confidence.

Clear Actions That Break the Fast

When done intentionally between dawn (Fajr) and sunset (Maghrib), certain acts invalidate the fast. Eating or drinking on purpose is the most obvious, as Allah commands in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:187) to refrain until nightfall. Intimacy between spouses during fasting hours is also prohibited, with clear rulings in authentic hadith. Deliberately inducing vomiting breaks the fast, while natural sickness does not. For women, menstruation or postnatal bleeding ends the fast automatically. In modern terms, nutritional IV drips or injections that feed the body are also considered to break the fast.

Actions That Do Not Break the Fast

Some acts are often misunderstood but are permitted. Eating or drinking by mistake does not harm the fast, as the Prophet ﷺ said, “It is Allah who has fed him and given him drink.” (Imam Muslim). Swallowing your own saliva is natural and unavoidable. Brushing teeth or using miswak is fine as long as nothing is swallowed. Using eye or ear drops is allowed according to many scholars. Even swimming or showering is fine provided you take precautions to avoid accidentally swallowing water, which can invalidate the fast.

Modern-Day Scenarios

With modern medicine, new situations arise. Non-nutritive injections like insulin or vaccines generally do not break the fast because they do not nourish the body through the digestive tract. Inhalers for asthma are debated, though many scholars allow them if medically necessary. Blood tests and oxygen therapy without nutritional additives do not invalidate fasting. If you face a unique situation, seek advice from a knowledgeable scholar who understands your health needs.

Accidental vs. Intentional Actions

If you accidentally break your fast — for example, taking a sip of water without realizing it’s daytime — your fast remains valid. No makeup is required. But intentionally breaking the fast without a valid reason is a serious matter. You must make up the day, and for certain acts, such as intimacy during fasting hours, you may also owe kaffarah, which can mean fasting 60 consecutive days.

Consequences and Making Up Missed Fasts

Deliberately breaking a fast should be met with repentance and corrective action. Depending on the act, you may need to make up the day (qada) or also give expiation (kaffarah).

Closing Reflection

Fasting is not only about avoiding food and drink — it is a discipline of the body, mind, and heart. By knowing what breaks the fast and what doesn’t, you can worship with clarity and peace. While the blessed month is unique, the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ encouraged voluntary fasting throughout the year to maintain a strong connection with Allah. Fasting after Ramadan helps preserve the discipline, worship, and closeness to Allah that we gained during those precious days.

As mentioned in Surah Al-Baqarah (2:183), fasting is prescribed to help believers attain taqwa (God-consciousness). Continuing to fast after Ramadan keeps that awareness alive in our daily lives.

Why Post-Ramadan Fasting Matters

Ramadan trains the soul to resist temptation, increase worship, and serve others. Continuing to fast after it ends keeps those habits strong. The Prophet ﷺ compared consistent worship to planting seeds and caring for them, rather than neglecting them after planting.

Post-Ramadan fasting brings forgiveness, extra reward, and steadiness in faith. It also helps maintain self-discipline, healthier habits, and spiritual focus.

Six Days of Shawwal Fasting

One of the most recommended acts after Ramadan is fasting six days in Shawwal, the month that directly follows Ramadan. The Prophet ﷺ said: “Whoever fasts Ramadan and then follows it with six days of Shawwal, it will be as if he fasted for a lifetime.” (Imam Muslim)

These days can be fasted consecutively or spread throughout the month. The key is to complete them within Shawwal to receive the promised reward.

For example, some families fast together on weekends for encouragement, while a student might spread their Shawwal fasts around exam days to manage energy. Preparing simple, nourishing meals for suhoor and iftar makes the fast easier to keep.

Weekly Sunnah Fasting (Monday & Thursday)

The Prophet ﷺ regularly fasted on Mondays and Thursdays, saying that deeds are presented to Allah on these days. This habit keeps a steady rhythm of worship and is a simple way to continue Ramadan’s spirit.

Combining Qada with Sunnah Fasts

If you have missed fasts from Ramadan, you can make them up (qada) while also earning the reward of Sunnah fasts, such as the Six Days of Shawwal. 

Imam Ramli Rahimahullahu Taala was once asked about making up missed fasts of Ramadan and whether the reward of the six sunnah fasts of Shawwal would still apply. He replied:

“The qadha of Ramadan is valid even if combined with another intention for fasting. Thus, it is permissible to combine it with the sunnah six days of Shawwal. This matter has also been affirmed by a number of contemporary scholars.”

Scholars differ on whether both intentions can be combined, so do consult your local scholars for further clarification if need be. 

Practical Tips for Fasting After Ramadan

  • Set clear goals: Decide which fasts you will observe and write them in your calendar.
  • Pair with other acts of worship: Read Qur’an, give sadaqah, or visit someone in need on fasting days.
  • Use reminders: The Muslim Pro app can help you set alerts for suhoor, iftar, and upcoming Sunnah fasts, as well as track both voluntary and qada fasts.
  • Stay consistent: Even one or two voluntary fasts a month keeps the habit alive and strengthens your connection with Allah.

Fasting after Ramadan is a gift we give ourselves, a way to hold onto the blessings we worked hard to earn. With clear planning, sincere intention, and consistent practice, these fasts can carry the light of Ramadan through the entire year.

]]> https://www.muslimpro.com/how-to-fast-after-ramadan-and-keep-faith-strong/feed/ 0 40443 Welcoming a Baby in Islam: A Guide for New Parents https://www.muslimpro.com/welcoming-a-baby-in-islam-a-guide-for-new-parents/ https://www.muslimpro.com/welcoming-a-baby-in-islam-a-guide-for-new-parents/#respond Mon, 15 Sep 2025 02:24:01 +0000 https://www.muslimpro.com/?p=40419 The birth of a child is one of life’s greatest blessings. In Islam, welcoming a baby is marked by beautiful Sunnah acts that combine gratitude, community, and faith. These traditions begin from the moment of birth and help lay the foundation for the child’s spiritual upbringing. Whether you are preparing for your first child or refreshing your knowledge, this guide shares the key Islamic birth traditions for new parents.

1. Recite the Adhan in the Baby’s Ear

It is Sunnah for the father, or another trusted family member, to softly recite the Adhan in the newborn’s right ear soon after birth. This ensures that the first words the baby hears are the call to worship, instilling the remembrance of Allah from the very beginning.

“Narrated AbuRafi’: I saw the Messenger of Allah (ﷺ) uttering the call to prayer (Adhan) in the ear of al-Hasan ibn Ali when Fatimah gave birth to him.” (Imam Abu Dawud)

2. Perform Tahneek

Tahneek involves gently rubbing a small amount of softened date (or a similar sweet fruit) on the baby’s palate. This was done by the Prophet Muhammad ﷺ for newborns, symbolising sweetness, blessing, and a connection to the Sunnah.

“Abu Musa reported: A child was born in my house and I brought him to Allah’s Apostle (may peace be upod him) and he gave him the name of Ibrahim and he rubbed his palate with dates.” (Imam Muslim)

3. Choose a Good Name

In Islam, names carry deep meaning. Parents are encouraged to choose names with positive meanings, such as those of the Prophets, righteous companions, or words with virtuous qualities. Avoid names with negative or inappropriate meanings.

4. Perform Aqiqah

Aqiqah is performed on the seventh day after the baby’s birth, or later if necessary. It involves sacrificing an animal, shaving the baby’s head, and giving charity equal to the weight of the hair in silver. This act expresses gratitude to Allah and shares blessings with others.

“Every child is in pledge for his Aqiqah, which is sacrificed for him on the seventh day, his head is shaved, and he is given a name.” (Imam Abu Dawud)

5. Shave the Baby’s Head

Shaving the baby’s head on the day of Aqiqah is Sunnah. It symbolises purification and a fresh start. The hair is weighed, and its weight in silver (or the monetary equivalent) is given in charity.

6. Circumcision

For boys, circumcision is part of the fitrah (natural disposition) and is recommended for hygiene and religious purposes. It can be done in infancy or later, depending on health and cultural practices.

“Five are from the fitrah: circumcision, shaving the pubic hair, clipping the nails, plucking the underarm hair, and trimming the moustache.” (Imam Al-Bukhari and Imam Muslim)

7. Make Du’a for the Child

Parents should make du’a for the child’s faith, well-being, and righteous character. A simple supplication is: “May Allah bless you, make you righteous, and guide you.” You can also use authentic duas from the Qur’an and Sunnah.

Note on Variations

Some of these practices may vary slightly between Islamic schools of thought and cultures. It is always best to follow the guidance of your local imam or scholar.

Final Thoughts

From the first Adhan to the joyful Aqiqah, Islamic birth traditions carry deep meaning and purpose. They strengthen the family’s connection to faith, bring blessings to the community, and remind parents of their responsibility in raising their child with love and remembrance of Allah.

The Prophet Muhammad ﷺ encouraged voluntary fasts throughout the year, each carrying unique blessings. These Sunnah fasts draw us closer to Allah, help us follow the Prophet’s example, and keep our hearts engaged in worship beyond Ramadan.

This guide outlines the main Sunnah days to fast, their virtues, and how to make them part of a consistent routine.

Why Sunnah Fasting Matters

Sunnah fasting refers to voluntary fasts performed in imitation of the Prophet ﷺ. They are a means of seeking Allah’s pleasure, purifying the soul, and multiplying rewards.

Abu Huraira (RA) reported that the Messenger of Allah ﷺ said: “Every deed of the son of Adam will be multiplied, a good deed receiving a tenfold to seven hundredfold reward. Allah the Exalted says: Except for fasting, for it is for Me and I will reward it.” (Imam Muslim)

Observing these fasts is an opportunity for forgiveness, spiritual growth, and closeness to Allah.

Weekly Sunnah Fasts (Monday and Thursday)

The Prophet ﷺ regularly fasted on Mondays and Thursdays. He said: “Deeds are presented [to Allah] on Mondays and Thursdays, and I like that my deeds be presented while I am fasting.” (Imam al-Tirmidhi)

Fasting on these days builds discipline and integrates regular worship into the weekly routine.

Monthly White Days (Ayyam al-Bid)

The Ayyam al-Bid, meaning “White Days,” are the 13th, 14th, and 15th days of each lunar month, named for the brightness of the full moon. Fasting on all three is reported to bring the reward of fasting the entire month.

Even fasting one or two of these days brings immense reward. Marking them in your calendar makes it easier to plan ahead.

Six Days of Shawwal

Fasting six days in Shawwal, after completing Ramadan, carries great merit. The Prophet ﷺ said: “Whoever fasts Ramadan and then follows it with six days of Shawwal, it will be as if he fasted for a lifetime.” (Imam Muslim)

Day of Arafah Fasting

For those not performing Hajj, fasting on the 9th of Dhul-Hijjah, the Day of Arafah, is one of the most rewarding voluntary fasts. The Prophet ﷺ said it expiates the sins of the previous year and the coming year.

It is a day for dua, remembrance, and gratitude.

Day of Ashura Fasting

The 10th of Muharram, the Day of Ashura, holds great spiritual significance. The Prophet ﷺ said fasting on this day erases sins of the past year. He encouraged fasting the day before or after alongside it.

What Fasting Means in Islam

In Islamic law, ṣawm means refraining from food, drink, and other things that break the fast from dawn (fajr) to sunset (maghrib) with a sincere intention to worship Allah. As mentioned in Surah Al-Baqarah, 2:183, fasting is prescribed so that believers may attain taqwa, or God-consciousness.

There are different kinds of fasting. Ramadan fasting is obligatory (fard). Sunnah fasts follow the example of the Prophet ﷺ. Sunnah fasts are extra acts of devotion, while expiatory (kaffarah) fasts are required for certain violations. The Muslim Pro app allows you to bookmark Qur’anic verses like 2:183 and listen to them during your fast for reflection.

Ramadan Fasting Rules

Fasting in Ramadan is required for every adult Muslim who is healthy, sane, and not traveling. Children are encouraged to start gradually so they can grow into the habit.

The intention (niyyah) should be made before fajr. While some scholars allow one intention for the whole month during Ramadan, many recommend renewing it daily. A simple thought in the heart is enough, but you may also say it verbally.

A fast is broken by deliberately eating or drinking, marital intimacy during the fasting hours, intentional vomiting, or taking nutritional IV drips. Accidental eating or drinking does not break the fast. Non-nutritive injections, blood tests, and the use of miswak are allowed. Toothpaste is permissible if nothing is swallowed.

Some people are exempt from fasting. This includes those who are ill, pregnant, breastfeeding, elderly, or traveling. Missed fasts should be made up (qada) before the next Ramadan. If fasting is permanently not possible, a fidya is given. In some cases, a kaffarah is required.

Sunnah Days to Fast

Fasting outside of Ramadan brings great reward. The Prophet ﷺ encouraged fasting on Mondays and Thursdays, and on the White Days — the 13th, 14th, and 15th of each lunar month.

Other blessed days include the six days of Shawwal, the Day of Arafah, and the Day of Ashura. For example, fasting on the Day of Arafah erases the sins of the previous and coming year for those not on Hajj (Muslim). Fasting on Ashura expiates the sins of the previous year (Imam Al-Bukhari & Imam Muslim). The Muslim Pro Hijri calendar can remind you of these dates so you never miss them.

Fasting After Ramadan

The six days of Shawwal are highly recommended and can be completed consecutively or spread throughout the month. Weekly Sunnah fasts, such as Mondays and Thursdays, help maintain the discipline built in Ramadan. Some scholars permit combining a qada fast with a Sunnah fast, though it is best to follow the opinion of your trusted local scholar.

Prohibited Fasting Days in Islam

Some days are prohibited for fasting. These include Eid al-Fitr, Eid al-Adha, and the Days of Tashreeq (the 11th to 13th of Dhul-Hijjah). Avoiding fasting on these days is part of honoring the blessings Allah has given.

Can I use toothpaste to brush my teeth while fasting?

Yes, you can use toothpaste to brush your teeth while fasting as long as you do not swallow it.

Do inhalers or CPAP machines break the fast?

In cases of medical need, they may be used, though scholars differ. Follow the opinion of your trusted scholar.

Does vaping or smoking break the fast?

Yes, both invalidate the fast. Ramadan is a good time to quit.

What if I eat or drink unintentionally by mistake?

Continue fasting if you unintentionally eat or drink by mistake. Your fast remains valid.

Can I use eye drops, ear drops, or nasal sprays?

Often they are allowed, but take care to avoid swallowing any liquid.

Do blood tests or injections break the fast?

Non-nutritive injections and blood tests do not break the fast.

How can I deal with headaches or caffeine withdrawal?

Hydrate well at night and gradually reduce caffeine before Ramadan.
